Associations between hospital chargemaster prices and regional health in the greater St Louis region

Of the health issues facing the United States today, healthcare costs remain at the center of attention. Such costs have more than tripled over the last 60 years, and are forecasted to quadruple by 2025, occupying a large portion of the US national budget, economy, and business sectors, but also affecting individual families (Sahadi, 2018). Healthcare costs are purported to occupy over 1/3 of an average household income per year (Emanuel et al., 2017)), and especially impact those living with chronic diseases, such as cancer, heart disease, obesity, and diabetes (NCCDPHP, 2019). Furthermore, higher healthcare costs are associated with poor mental health in populations at risk, such as the elderly (Vasiliadis, H.M., 2013), the disabled, those with pre-existing mental health conditions (Mackin, S., 2011), and those who are underinsured. (Unützer, J. et al, 2009; Collins, S. R., Bhupal, H.K., Doty, M.M., 2019). One method of analyzing healthcare costs on individuals is by analyzing hospital prices themselves. Although treacherous and not easy to find, these prices are sometimes accessible by hospital though access to the hospital’s chargemaster. The hospital “charge master” is a comprehensive list of all procedures and products offered to patients at a hospital. Chargemasters began being used after the rise of indemnity insurance products in the early 1950s, in a time when charges for hospital services more closely represented billed charges for individual services. As the health insurance industry matured to a fee for service model, hospitals developed a vastly inflated “chargemaster” model, in order to gain market leverage over Medicare and private insurance companies seeking to create affordable insurance models for its customers. This has created an environment where both healthcare providers insurance companies can profit immensely from medical services at the detriment of patients, especially those without insurance and forced to visit doctors out of network.1,2,3,4 Current research on chargemaster’s impact on patients examines only financial outcomes, but no research to date has explored its effects on mental or physical health outcomes.
